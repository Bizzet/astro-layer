# Creative Concept Formation

A creative concept is the **single sentence that captures the idea behind the design** — not what the client does, but the unexpected angle that makes this site worth looking at.

Run this before choosing an archetype. The concept drives the archetype, not the reverse.

---

## What a Concept Is

A concept creates productive tension between two things that don't normally belong together. It is:

- **Specific** — visual enough to make design decisions from
- **Surprising** — you couldn't guess it from the industry alone
- **Honest** — it serves the brand adjectives, not a random reference

A concept is NOT a list of adjectives. "Professional and modern" is not a concept. Neither is "warm and approachable." Those are brand attributes. A concept is the *lens* through which those attributes are expressed.

---

## The Formation Method

**Step 1 — Name the cliché.**
Write the most obvious design for this type of business in one sentence.

> "An organic bakery → earthy, warm browns, handwritten font, rustic texture."

**Step 2 — Find the unexpected parallel.**
Ask: *What other world has the same essential quality, but is never associated with this industry?*

> "Craftsmanship + precision + warmth also describes: fine watchmaking / rare book dealers / haute cuisine / scientific illustration."

**Step 3 — Form the tension sentence.**
Combine the business with the parallel. The sentence should feel slightly surprising, but immediately *right*.

> "This bakery should feel like the atelier of a Swiss instrument maker — every loaf a precision object."

**Step 4 — Test it.**
Does it pass these three checks?

1. Could you make a specific visual decision (color, type, layout) from this sentence?
2. Would someone who heard it understand *exactly* what to avoid?
3. Is it honest to the brand adjectives, or is it clever at the brand's expense?

If it fails any check, reframe.

---

## Good Concepts vs. Bad Concepts

| Bad | Good |
|---|---|
| "Professional and modern" | "A consulting firm with the visual restraint of a Japanese knife shop" |
| "Warm and welcoming" | "A family dentist that feels like a mid-century Scandinavian design museum — friendly through precision, not softness" |
| "Fresh and natural" | "An organic food brand with the color palette of a 1960s Penguin Books cover" |
| "Bold and energetic" | "A personal trainer's site designed as if by a Soviet sports propagandist from 1968" |
| "Clean and trustworthy" | "A B2B SaaS product with the diagrammatic clarity of an IKEA assembly manual" |
| "Luxurious and refined" | "A luxury hotel that feels like a Kyoto paper mill — quiet labor made visible" |
| "Dark and moody" | "A whiskey brand that looks like a 1940s film noir title sequence — atmosphere as product" |
| "Creative and fun" | "A children's education platform designed by someone who loved mid-century Eastern European animation" |

## How the Concept Drives Archetype Selection

Once the concept is formed, choose the archetype that best *executes* the concept — not the archetype that describes the industry.

> Concept: "A florist with the precision and silence of a botanical illustration archive"
> → Archetype: **Editorial** or **Minimal/Clean** — not Organic/Natural
> → Rationale: "botanical illustration archive" implies restraint, precision typography, warm off-white paper — Editorial delivers that; Organic/Natural gives warmth without the archival precision

The concept should *constrain* which archetypes are viable. If two archetypes both fit, the one that creates more tension with the expected choice is usually stronger.

---

## When to Override the Concept

If the user has very specific, non-negotiable visual requirements ("we need it to look like our competitor but better" or "our brand guidelines require X"), treat those as hard constraints that narrow the concept space rather than reasons to abandon concept-first thinking. Find the concept that fits within the constraints.

---

## Recording the Concept

The concept goes into the design brief as the first entry. It must be a single sentence. If it takes two sentences, it isn't sharp enough — combine or cut.

The concept also sets the **anti-patterns for this specific site** at the end of the brief. The concept makes clear what the site must never look like. Always derive the three anti-patterns directly from the concept.
